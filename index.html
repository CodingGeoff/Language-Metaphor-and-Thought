Chapter 11: Language, Metaphor and Thought

In this chapter, we shall examine the relationships between language and thought. You may ask: But why is there a “metaphor” in the title? Well, you will see that metaphor is on the one hand a language phenomenon, a figure of speech (修辞格), on the other hand a cognitive device, a figure of thought (思维方式). In this sense, metaphor serves as a link between language and thought.

In the first four sections, we are going to consider four questions one after another. They are:

  • What is the relationship between language and thought?
  • What is the relationship between language and the outside world?
  • What is the nature of thought?
  • And what is the nature of categorization (范畴化)?

We do not aim at providing you with one correct answer for each of the questions, for indeed cognitive scientists have not been able to come up with such an answer. The purpose of raising these questions is to call your attention to them and to help you see that they should not be taken for granted. In Section 5, we are going to take a fresh look at the already familiar figure of speech known as “metaphor”, where we shall see how deeply metaphor has influenced our conceptualization of the world. Finally Section 6 gives a summary of the whole chapter.

1. A Preliminary Question: The Relationship Between Language and Thought

Have you ever considered the question what the relationship between language and thought is? Perhaps only occasionally. Perhaps not even once. Why? Because the question is just so fundamental and basic and appears to be so straightforward that we tend to take it for granted. Well, all of us can speak at least one language, and all of us can think without too much difficulty in that language we speak—isn’t this simple enough?

But is it really that simple? Is it really a question not worthy of pursuing? In our childhood we were all experts at raising silly questions such as “What does the wind do when it doesn’t blow?” or “Where did I live before I was born?” or “Where will I live after I die?” As we grow older, we gradually lose that naivety and with it goes our curiosity about the most fundamental questions of the world, life and mankind. One such question is the relationship between language and thought. Does language reflect our thought? Does language organize our thought? Does language control our thought?

Over the centuries there have been linguists who have pondered over this question. Among them was Benjamin Lee Whorf. He perceived language as a pair of glasses with more or less warped lenses through which we view our surroundings (notice there is a metaphor at work here). This perception was sharpened by his work with a language that is very different from English—that of the Hopi, a tribe of Pueblo Indians living in Arizona. He suggested that different languages give their speakers different world views and he observed that the world view of the English departs most radically from that of the Hopi in their conceptualizations of space and time. This has come to be referred to as the Whorf hypothesis (伍尔夫假设). (See also: linguistic determinism and linguistic relativity (语言决定论和语言相对论 ))

For example, in English our concepts of space are so pervasive that we are able to transfer them almost totally to time. As a result, time is conceived as a space of strictly limited dimensions, or as a motion upon such a space. We also treat time as a mass and carve it into units and count them. That’s why we can say one second, two minutes, three hours, four days, five weeks, etc. We also use the same prepositions such as before, after, in, at, on for both space and time. The same is true with adjectives such as long, short, same, different, right, wrong, hard, nice, more, less, etc. Even some nouns are shared by space and time as well. Consider stretch of time, segment of time, amount of time, and you’ll see what I mean.

However, according to Whorf, this is almost never done in Hopi, where time is more accurately translated as a kind of “duration”. This “duration” seems to be inconceivable in terms of space or motion. To illustrate the differences between the English time and the Hopi “duration” with one example, let’s look at the sentence “Ten days is longer than nine days”. The Hopi expression of the same idea in terms of duration would be something like “The tenth day is later than the ninth”.

Whorf further pointed out that much that is difficult in recent physics as well as in philosophy and logic has been the struggle to climb out of the way of looking at the world which we inherit from our language. He even surmised that a world view such as that of the Hopi might be more congenial to the concepts of modern physics than the languages of Western Europe (Bolinger 1968: 253 - 255).

The Whorf hypothesis has aroused a series of debate among linguists, philosophers, and anthropologists. Nowadays it is generally felt that Whorf’s position was exaggerated. Nevertheless, the relationships between language and thought still remain a mystery worthy of further investigations. We hope that when you reach the end of this chapter, you will be able to form your own opinions on this issue.

2. What Does Language Reflect? The Relationship Between Language and the Outside World

Let us move on to another question which is related to the first one: What do you think is the relationship between language and the outside world? Does language mirror the outside world? Does language give a distorted picture of the outside world? Does language create the outside world? In this section, we shall see that the traditional linguistic approach and the modern cognitive approach have provided very different answers.

The Traditional Linguistic Approach

First let’s see what traditional linguistics has got to say on these questions. Basically, its main tenets can be summarized as the following three points (Gardenfors 1998, chapter 5, p. 2):

  1. Meaning exists in the objective world. That is to say, the meaning of a word or expression is something out there in the world. It is not something created by the human mind. The meaning of an expression is independent of how individual users understand it. For example, the meaning of the word table exists because in the objective world there is such an entity table. How individual users use the word table will not alter its meaning.
  2. Language derives meaning from reference to objects in this world. That is to say, the constituents of the language are mapped onto the outside world. Names are mapped onto objects, verbs are mapped onto sets of objects or relations between objects, adjectives are mapped onto properties of objects, etc. For example, the name book refers to the concrete entity book in the objective world; the verb rise captures the relationship between two objects when one is moving upward and the other remaining static; the adjective long captures the property shared by objects like a road, a train, or a string.
  3. The main objective of linguistic research is to formulate truth conditions for the sentences in the language because such conditions are supposed to determine the meaning of those sentences. Take the sentence “The apple is red” as an example. What is left for linguists to do is to work out under which conditions this sentence can be judged to be true, that is, under which conditions this sentence describes faithfully a state of affairs that exists in the outside world. Once that is found out, the meaning of the sentence is clear.

Well, how did you like these three claims? At least at the first sight, they look rather convincing and seem to have captured our intuition concerning language. Can you illustrate these points made by traditional linguistics with a figure?

All in all, this traditional linguistic approach ascribes to language, and to language users, a rather passive role: Everything is already out there in the world. What human beings have to do, and indeed what they can do, with their language, is to mirror that outside world as closely as possible.

The Modern Cognitive Approach

But are human beings really as passive as that? We shall see that an alternative approach, the modern cognitive approach, has come up with a rather different version of answer on the question what language reflects. Again we can summarize it along three lines (Gardenfors 1998, chapter 5, pp. 3 - 4):

  1. Meanings are mental entities, i.e., they are things in the head. To put it in another way, meanings are elements of a conceptual structure in the heads of the language users. This claim is in direct contrast with the claim made by traditional linguistics that meaning is something out there in the world.
  2. Language does not derive meaning from reference to objects in the outside world. Rather, language derives meaning from reference to a conceptualization of the world. In other words, what language represents is not the outside world, but a particular conceptualization of that world. Language may be said to represent the outside world only in an indirect way via a conceptualization.
  3. Language itself is part of the conceptual structure in the heads of the language users. It is not an entity with independent standing isolated from other mental faculties such as memory, perception, logical inferencing, etc. Rather it is intimately linked with these other mental faculties and as such mirrors the interplay of psychological, cultural, social, ecological, and other factors.

3. What Is the Nature of Thought? (思维的本质是什么?)

Let us move on to the third question: What is the nature of thought? To put it in Chinese, the question is 思维的本质是什么? This is again a rather philosophical question and you may be saying to yourself: Hey, we are talking about language here, and why shall we bother with philosophical investigations? Well, you will see that philosophy and language research are very much interrelated. On the one hand, some interesting language phenomena—including the Cooperative Principle and the speech act theory, which you may recall from Chapter 5—were first systematically studied by philosophers. On the other hand, linguists’ efforts in revealing the mysteries of human language have also shed light on a lot of philosophical issues. Every approach to language and thought must be guided by a particular philosophy, be it objectivism, subjectivism, or any other school.

In this section, we are going to compare two different answers, one provided by objectivism (客观主义) and the other by experiential realism (经验现实主义), on the question of the nature of thought.

Objectivism

Let us start with objectivism. Its main claims on this issue can be presented as the following three points:

  1. Thought is abstract in the sense that it is the manipulation of abstract symbols. For example, if A is larger than B and B is larger than C, than A is larger than C. Symbols get their meaning via correspondences to entities and categories in the world. In this way, the mind can represent external reality and be said to “mirror nature” (Lakoff 1987: 163).
  2. Thought is “disembodied” in the sense that it exists independently of the individual doing the thinking and is thus not determined by his/her properties. The nature of the human body and the way it functions are considered as irrelevant to the objectivist account of thought. In fact thought should transcend the limitations of any particular kind of being. It follows from this whether the human beings have a body that stands upright (as we do) or whether they have a body that lies front - back (as dogs do) will not leave any impact on how they conceive the world.
  3. Thought is objectively true or false. It is true and accurate when it corresponds to the outside world, that is, when the symbols used in thought correctly correspond to entities and categories in the world and when the mind reproduces the logical relations that exist objectively among the entities and categories of entities in the world. It is false when it fails to do so (Lakoff 1987: 163).

These fairly abstract claims about thought seem to capture our common sense well: Nobody can identify a thought as a concrete entity—in this sense it is indeed abstract. It is also very difficult to see what kind of role the human body plays in thought. Hence it is at least safer to hypothesize that the human body plays no part in thought than otherwise. Knowledge like “The earth moves around the sun” and “One plus one equals two” is indeed objectively true.

So what is left for challenge? Well, if we shift our eyes away from such commonsense assumptions about physical objects to other more abstract domains such as political movements, inflation, friendships, marriage, emotions, and foreign policies, we shall see that those claims do not necessarily hold. For example, when I say “I treasure the friendship between you and me”, am I doing the thinking about the friendship in an abstract way? Not exactly. I am actually treating friendship, an abstract concept, as a concrete entity that can be treasured, just like a precious gift. And if indeed human beings did not walk on their two legs but crawl like dogs on all fours, do you think that we would still say “The price goes up again”? Finally, when a man declares “I love you”, against what criteria are you going to judge that he is telling the truth?

Experiential Realism

On the basis of such considerations, experiential realism (Lakoff & Johnson 1980; Lakoff 1987; Johnson 1987) provides an alternative account for the nature of thought, which may be summarized as the following two points:

  1. Thought is “embodied”: It is grounded in our perception and determined by the characteristics of our body and our social environment (Nuyts 1993). For example, our understanding of UP - DOWN is determined by our physical make - up on the one hand and the gravity of the earth on the other hand. We grasp this structure in thousands of perceptions and activities we experience every day, such as perceiving a tree, our felt sense of standing upright, the activity of climbing stairs, forming a mental image of a flagpole, measuring our own heights, and experiencing the level of water rising in the bathtub. From these UP - DOWN experiences, images, and perceptions emerges the UP - DOWN structure.
  2. Thought is largely imaginative, based on the principles of metaphor and metonymy, and goes far beyond mirroring the outer world (ibid.). For example, in saying “He seems to be in high spirits these days”, an abstract concept, emotions, is understood via a less abstract concept, verticality, through a metaphor “Happiness Is Up”. In saying “I came across quite a few new faces at the meeting”, people are referred to as faces through a metonymy “Face Stands for Person”.

More and more linguists are coming to accept this account of thought provided by experiential realism and think it contains much truth. What do you think?

4. What Is the Nature of Categorization? (范畴化)

By categorization, we refer to the process by which people, in using language, necessarily group all the entities in the world around them. For example, whenever we use the word flower to refer to two different flowers, such as a rose and a violet, or describe two different objects, such as a tree and a flagpole, by the same word tall, we are undertaking acts of categorization. For although different, the two entities (rose vs. violet and tree vs. flagpole) are regarded in each case as the same.

Now why shall we bother with categorization? The reason is straightforward: Categorization is one of the most important cognitive activities of human beings. It is fundamental to all higher cognitive activity. Without categorization, we wouldn’t be able to come to terms with the world, for the input would be too enormous and too messy for us to handle. The essence of categorization lies in seeing sameness in difference.

Categorization has raised deep philosophical problems. There are many different accounts for categorization.

  • At one extreme, one group of philosophers claim that sameness is merely a matter of human invention. That is to say, those entities which may be called flowers, or which may be described as tall, really have nothing in common except for the names ascribed to them by human beings.
  • At the other extreme, another group of philosophers claim that categories like FLOWER and TALL exist independently of language and language users. They maintain that the words flower and tall merely name those categories that have already existed in the outside world prior to language.
  • In between these two extremes, there is an alternative position which postulates that “a word and the range of entities to which it may refer are mediated by a mental entity, i.e., a concept” (Taylor 1995: ix). That is to say, it is in virtue of a speaker’s knowledge of the concepts FLOWER and TALL that he/she is able to categorize different entities as flowers or as being tall.

To understand the nature of categorization, we need to consider the following questions:

  • Do categories have any basis in the real world, or are they merely constructs of the human mind?
  • What is the internal structure of categories?
  • How are categories learnt?
  • How do people assign entities to a category?
  • What kinds of relationships exist among categories?

(Taylor 1995: 1 - 2)

In the remaining part of this section, we are going to have a brief look at two different accounts of these questions, one known as the objectivist view and the other known as the prototype theory. But before we do that, you might like to go over in your mind your own understanding of categorization. For example, how will you define the category BOY? And BIRD? And FISH?

4.1 The Objectivist View of Categorization

This approach to categorization goes back ultimately to Greek antiquity, esp. to Aristotle, who distinguished between the essence of a thing and its accidents. The essence is that which makes a thing what it is. Accidents are incidental properties, which play no part in determining what a thing is. To cite one of his examples: The essence of man is “two - footed animal” (you don’t have to agree with this). Other properties, like being white or yellow, being cultured or barbarian, are accidental. They might be true of a certain individual, but they are irrelevant in determining whether an entity is indeed a man.

Over the centuries this classical approach to categorization has been developed and modified, but its domination in psychology, philosophy, and linguistics has rarely been challenged. Its basic assumptions can be summarized as the following four points (Taylor 1995: 23 - 24):

  1. Categories are defined in terms of a conjunction of necessary and sufficient features.

    It follows from this that when we assign an entity X to the category Y, we do this by checking the properties of X against the set of features which together define the essence of the category Y. To return to the category MAN, Aristotle singled out two defining features for it, one being [two - footed], the other being [animal]. Individually, these two features are necessary for the definition of the category; if either of the two is not possessed by the entity X, then X cannot be said to be a member of the category MAN. Jointly, the two features are sufficient; that is to say, so long as an entity exhibits each of the two, it can safely be judged as a member of the category MAN.

  2. Features are binary.

    This amounts to saying that a feature is either present or absent. It can take on only one of two values, i.e., either [+] or [-]. In any given instance, a feature is either involved in the definition of a category, or it is not; and an entity either possesses this feature, or it does not. To take the category BOY as an example, one of its defining features is [male]. An entity is either [+male] or [-male]. There can be no other possibilities.

  3. Categories have clear boundaries.

    By this we mean that an entity is either a member of a category or it is not. A category, once established, divides the universe into two sets of entities—those that are members of the category, and those that are not. There do not exist ambiguous cases where an entity “in a way” or “to some extent” belongs to the category, but in another way does not. For example, a person is either a member of the category BOY or he is not. We cannot say that by this criterion he is and by that criterion he is not.

  4. All members of a category have equal status.

    This is to say that there are no degrees of membership in a category, i.e., there are no entities which are better members of the category than others. All those entities which possess all the defining features of a category can be judged as full members of that category; in this sense they are equal to each other. And all those entities which do not possess all the defining features cannot be judged as members; in this sense they are also equal to each other. According to this view, we cannot say that a swallow is a better example of the category BIRD than a penguin. They are just both birds.

All these claims sound quite convincing and agree with our intuition. Now let’s investigate the categories of BOY and BIRD in some detail. Can you come up with a set of necessary and sufficient features for each of them?

BOY

  • +male
  • +unmarried
  • +not yet a father
  • ? to ? years old
  • etc.

BIRD

  • +having wings
  • +having a beak
  • +having feather
  • +able to fly
  • +able to lay eggs
  • +able to hatch eggs
  • etc.

Have you noticed the difficulties with the traditional view of categorization? Yes, there appear to be at least the following difficulties:

  1. As BOY and BIRD have shown, it is sometimes impossible to list all the common properties shared by the members of a category.
  2. Not all the members of a category may share a set of common properties. For this let’s think of the category FURNITURE. An experiment by Rosch (1975) has found out that chairs, dressers, pianos, mirrors, televisions and even pictures can all be counted as furniture in the eyes of Americans. But it is almost impossible to find a property that is shared by all of them.
  3. Not all features are binary, that is to say, not all features are a matter of all or none. Take [+able to fly] for BIRD as an example. What about a swallow who was seriously hurt in one of its wings and can only fly in a very awkward way? Is it able to fly or is it not?
  4. Not all the categories have a clear - cut boundary. For example, it can be difficult to judge if a 35 - year - old bachelor is a boy or not.
  5. Not all members of a category have equal status. For example, a 15 - year - old unmarried boy is a better example of BOY than a 35 - year - old bachelor.

4.2 The Prototype Theory of Categorization (原型论)

Many of the inadequacies of the classical theory of categorization that we have just reviewed were anticipated by Ludwig Wittgenstein in a frequently quoted passage in his Philosophical Investigations, where he addressed the question of how to define the category GAME. He notes that the various members of this category, such as board - games, card - games, ball - games, etc., do not share a set of common properties on whose basis games can be clearly distinguished from non - games. This observation has two implications. First, the boundary of the category is fuzzy rather than clear - cut. Second, contrary to the expectations of the classical theory, the category GAME is not structured in terms of shared necessary and sufficient features, but rather by a criss - crossing network of similarities. This is not to deny that “there are indeed attributes typically associated with the category. Some members share some of these attributes, other members share other attributes. However there are no attributes common to all the members, and to them alone. It may even be the case that some members have practically nothing in common with others.” (Taylor 1995: 38) Wittgenstein used the metaphor of a family resemblance to describe the structure of GAME, “for the various resemblances between members of a family: build, features, color of eyes, gait, temperament, etc., overlap and criss - cross in the same way” (Wittgenstein 1978: 33).

This state of affairs is by no means peculiar to the category GAME. Arguably, the majority of categories are structured in a similar way to this. Based on these observations and recognizing the difficulties with the classical theory, philosophers and linguists have put forward an alternative account for categorization, which is termed the prototype theory (原型论).

The term prototype can be roughly understood as the central member, or perhaps the cluster of central members, of a category. The main idea of the prototype theory can be summarized as follows:

  1. Categories are defined in terms of a family of resemblance rather than by means of a set of necessary and sufficient features.
  2. Membership in a category is determined by the perceived distance of resemblance of the entity to the prototype.
  3. Membership is not a matter of all or none.

For example, experiments show that robins and squirrels are conceived of as very close to the prototype for the category of BIRD. Cocks and penguins, on the other hand, are conceived of as much farther away from the prototype. That explains why the average language user considers cocks and penguins as less representative of the concept of being a bird. A large number of experiments have proved the psychological reality of the prototype structure of categories. For example, it has been found that chairs, sofas, and tables, and not mirrors, shelves, and clocks, are prototypical articles of furniture; it has also been found that murder is a better instance of killing than sacrificing. But why? Where do prototypes come from? One of the explanations offered is that prototype categories have a greater efficiency than classical categories.

Prototype categories have a flexibility, unknown to Aristotelian categories, in being able to accommodate new, hitherto unfamiliar data. With only Aristotelian categories at our disposal, new data would often demand, for their categorization, the creation of new categories, or a redefinition of existing categories. On the other hand, new entities and new experiences can be readily associated, perhaps as peripheral members, to a prototype category, without necessarily causing any fundamental restructuring of the category system. (Taylor 1995: 53)

In this respect, prototype categories achieve the flexibility required by an ever - changing environment.

To sum up, so far in this chapter we have briefly touched upon the Whorf hypothesis concerning the relationship between language and thought. We have also compared the traditional approach and the modern cognitive approach towards the questions what language reflects, what is the nature of thought and what is the nature of categorization. In the following section, we are going to shift our eyes to conceptual metaphor (概念隐喻), where you will see that metaphor serves as a bridge between language and thought.

5. Conceptual Metaphor (概念隐喻)

I bet you all understand the meaning of metaphor. Yes, it refers to a figure of speech which enables us to talk about one thing as if it were another. For example, in Burns’ famous lyric My Love Is a Red Red Rose, the poet’s sweetheart is compared to a rose. Where do you think we are most likely to come across a lot of metaphors? You might say, “Well, in literary works, esp. in poems. Those writers are great inventors of metaphors.” Why do you think people use metaphors in their language? You might say, “Well, perhaps it is for some aesthetic purposes. Metaphors are a useful decorative device. They can add a lot of flavor to one’s language.”

Everything seems to be fairly clear. But is it really as simple as that? Before you make a hasty answer, I would like to call your attention to the following examples:

  • 上一代/下一代
  • 上次/下次
  • 上半年/下半年
  • 上司/下属
  • 上流社会/下流社会
  • 上级部门/下级部门
  • 温度上升/温度下降
  • 价格上涨/价格下跌
  • 上班/下班
  • 上岗/下岗
  • 上课/下课

Now these appear to be very common expressions we use every day. Can you observe any metaphors in them? “Of course not” might be your answer. But look at 上一代 and 下一代. Is an older generation really located above you? And is a younger generation really located below you? No. So what are we doing here? We are doing a metaphorical thinking. We are giving a non - spatial domain, that of TIME, an up - down orientation. To be more specific, the metaphor that is at work here is something like “Earlier in time is shang/Later in time is xia”. We call metaphorical thinking like this conceptual metaphors. They underlie metaphorical expressions which we can notice in language, although we usually remain unaware of the existence of conceptual metaphors themselves.

Now can you work out the conceptual metaphors underlying the other three groups of examples? It shouldn’t be too difficult. They are:

  • Of a more important social status is shang.
  • Of a less important social status is xia.
  • Of a larger quantity is shang.
  • Of a smaller quantity is xia.
  • Of a more intensive state is shang.
  • Of a less intensive state is xia.

At this point some of you may be saying to yourselves, “Well, all this sounds nice. But I belong to that type of people who lack imagination and who have never created any metaphors. So far I survive.” OK, let’s have a try and see if we can indeed survive without resorting to metaphors. Let’s talk about time. To get around metaphorical expressions like 上一代 and 下一代, perhaps we can say 前辈 and 后辈? So far so good—but don’t you see you are caught again? What is 前辈? And what is 后辈? An older generation is not in front of you, nor is a younger generation behind you. You haven’t shredded off the metaphorical thinking which you are trying to avoid. This time instead of giving TIME an up - down orientation, you are giving it a front - back one.

The point here is once your attention is aroused, you will realize that metaphor is everywhere—not only are literary works full of metaphors, but our daily utterances as well. Underlying metaphorical expressions in our daily language, there is a huge system of conceptual metaphors which organizes our thinking. This system works automatically and continuously without our being aware of its existence. In this sense, we say we live by metaphors.

Now all this sounds very different from the rhetorical approach to metaphor that we are familiar with. We call this new approach the cognitive approach to metaphor. Its main claims can be summarized as the following five points:

  1. Metaphor is ubiquitous. It is a prevalent phenomenon in ordinary language. Metaphor in literature is not a distinctly different phenomenon from metaphor in ordinary language.

    Look at the following two groups of examples. Can you work out the conceptual metaphors underlying those utterances?

    Group 1:

    • Is that the foundation for your theory?
    • The theory needs more support.
    • The argument is shaky.
    • The argument collapsed.

    Group 2:

    • What he said left a bad taste in my mouth.
    • I just can’t swallow that claim.
    • That argument smells fishy.
    • Let me stew over that for a while.

    (Lakoff & Johnson 1980: 4)

    Underlying the first group of examples, there is the conceptual metaphor “Theories are buildings”. This metaphor enables us to understand theory, a fairly abstract concept, through building, a more concrete one. Hence we can talk about theory as if it had a foundation, needed support, could be shaky and could even collapse.

    Underlying the second group of examples, there is the conceptual metaphor “Ideas are food”. With this metaphor, we talk about the taste and the smell of ideas. We even swallow or cook a certain idea as if it were food. What I would also like to emphasize is that the utterances in both groups of examples are taken from everyday conversations, not from literary works. This offers support for the claim that everyday utterances are full of metaphors just like literary works.

  2. Metaphor is conceptual in nature. It is not a figure of speech, a linguistic object. It is more accurately a conceptual organization expressed by the linguistic object.

    To explicate this point, let us examine the following group of examples. Can you work out the conceptual metaphor at work here?

    • Your claims are indefensible.
    • He attacked every weak point in my argument.
    • His criticisms were right on target.
    • I demolished his argument.
    • I’ve never won an argument with him.

    (Lakoff & Johnson 1980: 4)

    This time the metaphor is “Argument is war”. What is to be noticed is that we do not just talk about arguments in terms of war, but we do our arguing and construe the concept ARGUMENT through the concept of war. Thus we see the person we are arguing with as an opponent; we defend our own positions and attack his/hers; we gain ground when we win arguments and lose ground when losing arguments; we also plan and use strategies while arguing. It is in this sense that we say the conceptual metaphor “Argument is war” structures what we do and how we understand what we are doing when we argue. To put it in another way, we talk about arguments in terms of war because we conceive of arguments through our concept of war, and we act according to the way we conceive of things.

    To appreciate this point better, let us try to imagine a culture where an argument, instead of being viewed in terms of war, is viewed as a dance. Accordingly, the participants of an argument are seen as performers rather than fighters, and the goal is to perform in a balanced and aesthetically pleasing way rather than to win. People from such a cultural background would view, experience, carry out and talk about arguments in a different way from the English, and the difference would arise from the two different conceptual metaphors: “Argument is war” versus “Argument is dance”.

    Not only is the concept ARGUMENT structured by the metaphor “Argument is war”, but many other abstract concepts like LIFE, LOVE, IDEAS, THEORIES are structured by conceptual metaphors like “Life is a play”, “Love is a journey”, “Ideas are food”, “Theories are buildings”. In brief, metaphor is not just a linguistic phenomenon, it is conceptual in nature; metaphorical expressions are possible because there exist metaphorical concepts in our conceptual system.

  3. Metaphor is systematic. One common metaphorical concept may give rise to numerous linguistic expressions; different metaphorical concepts form a coherent network which underlies both our speech and our thoughts.

    Conceptual metaphors do not operate in isolation from each other. Rather “metaphorical entailments can characterize a coherent system of metaphorical concepts and a corresponding coherent system of metaphorical expressions for those concepts” (Lakoff & Johnson 1980: 9). The systematicity of metaphor is reflected in three conceptual metaphors of TIME, as the following examples show:

    Time is money.

    • How do you spend your time these days?
    • That flat tire cost me an hour.

    Time is a limited resource.

    • You don’t use your time profitably.
    • You are running out of time.

    Time is a valuable commodity.

    • I don’t have the time to give you.
    • Thank you for your time.

    (Lakoff & Johnson 1980: 7 - 8)

    “Time is money”, “Time is a limited resource”, and “Time is a valuable commodity” are all metaphorical concepts in that they capture the way we use our everyday experiences with money, limited resources, and valuable commodities to conceptualize time. Furthermore, together they form a coherent system since in our society money is a limited resource, which is in turn a valuable commodity. We might say that “Time is money” entails that “Time is a limited resource”, which entails that “Time is a valuable commodity”.

  4. Metaphor is composed of two domains, a relatively more clearly structured source domain (始源域) and a relatively less clearly structured target domain. The structure of a metaphor involves a mapping of the source domain’s schematic structure onto the target domain.

    Examples include “Life is a play” (as used by Shakespeare) and the everyday conceptual metaphor “Life is a journey.” For "LIFE IS A JOURNEY", specific correspondences include:

    Source Domain (JOURNEY) Target Domain (LIFE)
    traveler person
    point of departure birth
    initial conditions personal endowment
    baggage personal problems
    obstacles external difficulties
    distance covered accomplishments
    destination life purpose
    termination death
  5. Metaphor is grounded in bodily experience. Metaphorical mappings are not arbitrary but are grounded in bodily experience. Once established, they can impose structure on real life and create new real-life correspondences. For example, the conceptual metaphor “More is up” arises from common experiences like pouring more fluid into a container and seeing the level go up (a correspondence between quantity and verticality).

    The experiential bases of metaphors and their realizations are both correlations in real-life experiences that share the same structure as the metaphors. Experiential bases precede and ground conventional metaphors, while realizations follow. What is a realization for one generation can become part of the experiential basis for the next.

    Lakoff (1993) lists several ways in which metaphors can become real: through man-made objects designed according to the metaphor (e.g., thermometers for "More is up"), through imaginative products (e.g., cartoons depicting anger as steam from the ears for "Anger is a hot fluid in a container"), through physical symptoms, through social institutions (e.g., the design of halls in the Forbidden City where height symbolizes status, reflecting "High status is up"), etc. No clear distinction exists between experiential bases and realizations.

In summary, this section demonstrates metaphor’s prevalence, conceptual nature, systematicity, internal structure, and experiential grounding. Metaphor is fundamentally conceptual, not just linguistic. Metaphorical expressions are surface manifestations of a vast system of conceptual metaphors. As a cognitive device, metaphor helps us understand abstract concepts via more concrete ones. Structurally, it’s a one-directional mapping from a source to a target domain, grounded in bodily experience. The conceptual metaphor system operates unconsciously, automatically, and constantly, playing a major role in our language and conceptualization.

6. Summary

This chapter begins with the question, “What is the relationship between language and thought?” At one extreme, some people think that language passively reflects our thought. At the other extreme, some people (represented by B. L. Whorf) argue that language controls our thought.

Closely related is the question “What does language reflect?” Traditional linguistics holds that language mirrors the outside world directly. Cognitive linguistics, however, claims that language reflects a human conceptualization of the outside world.

These linguistic trends also differ on “What is the nature of thought?” Traditional linguists see thought as abstract and disembodied; cognitive linguists view it as embodied and largely imaginative.

For categorization, traditional linguistics follows Aristotle, defining categories by a conjunction of necessary and sufficient features. Cognitive linguistics adopts the prototype theory, defining categories by a family of resemblance around one or several prototypes.

Finally, as demonstrated in the chapter, metaphor is ubiquitous, conceptual in nature, systematic, composed of a source domain and a target domain, and grounded in bodily experience.
